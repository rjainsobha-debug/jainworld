The 24 Tirthankaras

Ford-makers of the current cosmic cycle (Avasarpiṇī). Each Tirthankara attained Kevala Jnana (omniscience) and established the fourfold Jain Sangha.

The word Tirthankara (तीर्थंकर) means "ford-maker" — one who creates a path across the ocean of worldly existence. They are the supreme spiritual teachers who revive the Jain path of liberation in each cosmic cycle.

Lord Mahavira — The 24th Tirthankara

Vardhamana Mahavira (Sanskrit: वर्धमान महावीर), born in 599 BCE in the ancient city of Vaishali (present-day Bihar, India), was the 24th and last Tirthankara of the current cosmic age. He is considered the greatest spiritual reformer in Jainism and is primarily responsible for the widespread revival and systematic organization of Jain philosophy as we know it today.

Born into the Kshatriya warrior caste to King Siddhartha and Queen Trishala, Mahavira showed signs of extraordinary spiritual prowess from childhood. The name Mahavira (Great Hero) was given later in his life, recognizing his supreme courage and spiritual triumph over the senses.

Early Life and Birth

According to Jain texts, Queen Trishala experienced fourteen auspicious dreams before Mahavira's birth — dreams interpreted by astrologers as heralding the birth of either a universal emperor (Chakravartin) or a Tirthankara. He was born on the 13th day of the bright half of Chaitra month (celebrated today as Mahavir Jayanti).

Mahavira grew up in the royal household, was well-educated in the arts, sciences, and Vedic traditions, and married Princess Yashoda (in some traditions). He had a daughter named Anojja. Despite palace comforts, he remained deeply drawn to spiritual inquiry, observing the suffering of all living beings around him.

Renunciation (Diksha)

At the age of 30, after his parents passed away, Mahavira renounced all worldly possessions — his palace, family, clothes, and even hair (which he pulled out with his hands, a practice called kesh-lochan). He took Diksha (initiation into ascetic life) and began twelve and a half years of intense spiritual practice.

During this period he:

  • Maintained complete silence for most of his ascetic life
  • Endured extreme heat, cold, and physical hardship without complaint
  • Practiced severe meditation and fasting
  • Walked barefoot through forests, villages, and cities
  • Never harmed any living being — even microorganisms
  • Bore attacks and insults from villagers with equanimity

Attainment of Kevala Jnana (Omniscience)

After twelve and a half years of intense penance, Mahavira attained Kevala Jnana — perfect, complete, infinite knowledge — on the 10th day of Vaishakha (around 557 BCE), under a Sala tree on the banks of the Rijupalika River near the village of Jrimbhikagrama. This is the highest state achievable in Jain spiritual philosophy, signifying total liberation from karma.

What is Kevala Jnana?

Kevala Jnana is the direct, simultaneous, and complete perception of all substances, all modes, all time (past, present, future), and all space — without any obstruction. It is not a gift from God but the natural state of the pure soul, revealed when all karmic particles are destroyed.

Teachings of Mahavira

After attaining Kevala Jnana, Mahavira spent the remaining 30 years of his life preaching across eastern India. His core message can be summarized in a few fundamental principles:

The Five Mahavrata (Great Vows) for Monks

  • Ahimsa (अहिंसा) — Complete non-violence in thought, word, and deed
  • Satya (सत्य) — Absolute truthfulness
  • Asteya (अस्तेय) — Non-stealing
  • Brahmacharya (ब्रह्मचर्य) — Celibacy
  • Aparigraha (अपरिग्रह) — Complete non-possessiveness

Twelve Anuvrata (Lesser Vows) for Laypersons

For householders, Mahavira prescribed twelve lesser vows adapted to the constraints of ordinary life, including partial non-violence, limited possessions, and periodic fasting.

The Doctrine of Anekantavada

Mahavira emphasized that reality is multifaceted and cannot be captured by any single point of view. This doctrine of Anekantavada (many-sidedness of truth) is Jainism's great contribution to world philosophy and epistemology.

Nirvana and Legacy

At the age of 72, Mahavira attained Nirvana (liberation) at Pawapuri, Bihar, on the night of Kartika Krishna Chaturdashi — the very night that many Hindus observe as Diwali. Jains observe this as the sacred festival of Diwali, marking not celebration of wealth, but the liberation of the great soul.

His chief disciples (Ganadharas), including Indrabhuti Gautama, compiled his teachings into the Jain Agamas, the canonical scriptures of Jainism.

Associated Sacred Places

  • Vaishali, Bihar — Birthplace (Kundagrama)
  • Jrimbhikagrama, Bihar — Place of Kevala Jnana
  • Pawapuri, Bihar — Place of Nirvana (Moksha)
  • Rajgir, Bihar — Major preaching ground
  • Shravanabelagola, Karnataka — Major pilgrimage site
  • Palitana, Gujarat — Sacred hill with 900+ temples

Rishabhanatha — The 1st Tirthankara

📍 1st Tirthankara Adinatha Digambara
Rishabhanatha (Adinatha)
🐂
Order1st
SymbolBull (Vrishabha)
ColorGolden
YakshaGomukha
YakshiChakreshvari
Key SiteAshtapada, Ayodhya

Rishabhanatha (Sanskrit: ऋषभनाथ), also known as Adinatha (First Lord), is the first Tirthankara of the present cosmic cycle. He is said to have lived in an ancient age when humanity was in a transitional period from a more perfect state of existence to the current imperfect one.

Rishabhanatha is credited with teaching humanity the basics of civilization — including agriculture, fire-making, cooking, pottery, and writing. He is revered not just as a spiritual teacher but as the founder of civilized society itself.

Significance

According to Jain cosmology, Rishabha appeared at the junction of two cosmic ages (the Tretayuga transition), when humans no longer had wish-fulfilling trees (Kalpavrikshas) to provide all their needs. He taught the 72 arts and crafts to men and the 64 arts to women to help humanity survive and prosper.

Teachings

  • Non-violence as the highest dharma
  • Renunciation of worldly attachments
  • The fourfold Sangha (monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen)
  • The path of liberation through right faith, right knowledge, right conduct

Mention in Hindu Texts

Remarkably, Rishabha (Rishaba) is also mentioned in the Bhagavata Purana (one of the 18 major Puranas), where he is described as an avatar of Vishnu. This has led some scholars to suggest a shared ancestry between Jain and Vedic traditions. Jains, of course, do not accept the avatar interpretation.

Sacred Sites

  • Ashtapada (Kailash) — Attained Nirvana here
  • Ayodhya, UP — Birthplace
  • Palitana, Gujarat — Major temple complex
  • Shikharji (Parasnath), Jharkhand — Important pilgrimage

Jain Literature

A vast treasury of Agamas, canonical texts, philosophical treatises, poetry, and folk literature spanning over 2,500 years.

Jain Agamas (Canonical Scriptures)

The Agamas are the primary canonical literature of Jainism, containing the teachings of Mahavira as compiled by his chief disciples (Ganadharas). Shvetambaras recognize 45 texts; Digambaras believe the original Agamas are lost.

  • Anga Agama · Ardhamagadhi
    Acharanga Sutra (आचारांग सूत्र)
    The oldest and most important Agama, dealing with the conduct of Jain monks and the biography of Mahavira. Contains the 15 chapters on ascetic discipline.
    1st AngaShvetambara Canon
  • Anga Agama · Ardhamagadhi
    Sutrakritanga (सूत्रकृतांग)
    The second Anga, dealing with the refutation of other philosophical doctrines and the path of liberation. Discusses the nature of the soul and karma.
    2nd AngaShvetambara Canon
  • Anga Agama · Ardhamagadhi
    Sthananga (स्थानांग)
    Arranged numerically, this text discusses various philosophical, cosmological, and ethical matters organized by number (1 to 10).
    3rd Anga
  • Upanga Agama
    Uvavai (Aupapatika Sutra)
    Describes the lives and conditions of various beings in different planes of existence. Contains the famous description of Mahavira's first sermon after attaining Kevala Jnana.
    1st Upanga

Philosophical Texts

  • Philosophy · Sanskrit
    Tattvartha Sutra by Umasvati
    The only Jain text accepted by all sects. Written in concise Sanskrit sutras, it covers the entire Jain worldview in 10 chapters — from the nature of the soul to liberation.
    ~200 CEAccepted by all sects
  • Philosophy · Prakrit
    Samayasara by Kundakunda
    A profound Digambara text exploring the pure nature of the soul (Paramatman) beyond all worldly classifications. Considers one of the most spiritually advanced Jain texts.
    ~100 CEDigambara

Poetry & Devotional Literature

  • Devotional Poetry · Apabhramsha
    Navkar Mantra — The Universal Jain Prayer
    The most sacred mantra in Jainism, saluting the five supreme beings (Pancha Parameshthi). Contains no specific deity's name — it pays respect to qualities, not persons.
    AncientAll Jain Sects

Jain Culture & Traditions

Festivals, rituals, lifestyle, diet, and the living traditions of the Jain community across India and the world.

🪔
Paryushan Mahaparva
The most sacred Jain festival, lasting 8 days (Shvetambara) or 10 days (Digambara, called Das Lakshana). A time of fasting, prayer, confession, forgiveness, and spiritual renewal. Ends with Samvatsari — the day of seeking universal forgiveness.
🎉
Mahavir Jayanti
The birth anniversary of Lord Mahavira, celebrated on Chaitra Shukla 13 (March–April). One of the most important Jain holidays, marked by processions, temple worship, and charity. Declared a national holiday in India.
🌙
Diwali — Liberation of Mahavira
While much of India celebrates Diwali as the festival of lights, Jains observe it as the day Lord Mahavira attained Nirvana (527 BCE). The lamps symbolize the light of knowledge that extinguished after the Great Soul's liberation.
🙏
Samvatsari — Universal Forgiveness
The holiest day of the Jain year, on which Jains seek forgiveness from all beings — known and unknown — for any harm caused in thought, word, or deed during the past year. The greeting: "Michhami Dukkadam."
🥗
Jain Diet (Jain Ahara)
Jains observe strict vegetarianism that goes beyond veganism — avoiding root vegetables (potatoes, onions, garlic), eating before sunset, avoiding certain fruits and vegetables during specific periods. The diet is based on minimizing harm to living beings.
🏔️
Jain Pilgrimage (Teertha)
Key Jain pilgrimage sites include Palitana (Gujarat), Shikharji (Jharkhand), Shravanabelagola (Karnataka), Pawapuri (Bihar), and Mount Abu (Rajasthan). Pilgrimage is considered a spiritually purifying act.

Jain Diet — Detailed Guide

The Jain diet is based on the principle of Ahimsa — minimal harm to all living beings. It is not merely vegetarian, but a carefully calibrated system that considers the number of senses each organism possesses.

What Jains Avoid

  • All meat, fish, poultry, and eggs
  • Root vegetables: potatoes, onions, garlic, carrots, beets, radishes (they contain many lives and cause death to the plant)
  • Eating after sunset (to avoid harming insects attracted to light)
  • Multi-seeded vegetables like brinjal (eggplant), figs (in some traditions)
  • Alcohol and other intoxicants
  • Honey (each drop contains infinite life forms)

Jain Fasting Traditions

  • Upvas — Complete fast (water only)
  • Ekasana — One meal a day
  • Biyasana — Two meals a day
  • Attham — Three-day fast
  • Masaksaman — One-month fast
  • Varshi Tapa — Alternate day fasting for one year (13 months)

Jain Philosophy

One of the world's most sophisticated and complete philosophical systems — built on direct perception, logical rigor, and ethical precision.

☮️
Ahimsa
अहिंसा — Non-violence
The supreme principle of Jainism. Non-violence in thought, word, and action towards all living beings. Recognized as the root of all virtues and the path to liberation. Mahatma Gandhi's ahimsa was directly inspired by Jain principles.
🔮
Anekantavada
अनेकान्तवाद — Many-sidedness of Truth
Reality is complex and multifaceted. No single statement or perspective can fully capture truth. This doctrine advocates intellectual humility and respect for other viewpoints. Expressed through Syadvada (conditional predication) and Nayavada (standpoints).
⚖️
Karma
कर्म — Action and Consequence
In Jain philosophy, karma is a physical substance — fine particles of matter — that attach to the soul based on actions, thoughts, and speech. The soul must shed all karma to achieve liberation. Jain karma theory is the most detailed in any philosophical tradition.
✨
Moksha
मोक्ष — Liberation
The ultimate goal of Jain existence — complete liberation of the soul from the cycle of birth and death (Samsara). A liberated soul (Siddha) dwells at the apex of the universe (Siddhashila) in a state of perfect knowledge, bliss, power, and perception.
🌱
Aparigraha
अपरिग्रह — Non-possessiveness
The principle of limiting possessions and desires. For monks: total non-possessiveness. For laypeople: defined limits on material possessions. This principle underpins the Jain emphasis on ethical business conduct and philanthropy.
👁️
Three Ratnas
त्रिरत्न — Three Jewels
The three foundations of the path to liberation: Samyak Darshana (Right Perception/Faith), Samyak Jnana (Right Knowledge), and Samyak Charitra (Right Conduct). Together they constitute the Jain path of liberation.
🌊
Samsara
संसार — Cycle of Existence
The soul's endless cycle of birth, death, and rebirth across the universe's various planes of existence. Liberation (Moksha) is the permanent escape from this cycle. Jainism identifies 8.4 million species of living beings in which the soul can be reborn.
🧘
Jiva & Ajiva
जीव–अजीव — Soul and Non-Soul
Jain metaphysics divides all reality into Jiva (conscious, living souls) and Ajiva (non-conscious matter, space, time, etc.). The interaction between the two — especially the binding of karma-matter to the soul — is the cause of Samsara.

Paryushan Mahaparva 2025 Celebrations Begin Across India with Record Participation

📅 April 18, 2025 📍 Pan-India By JanhWorld Desk

The most sacred festival in the Jain calendar, Paryushan Mahaparva, began today across hundreds of temples and Jain centers throughout India, with authorities reporting record participation this year. From Mumbai's Malabar Hill to Ahmedabad's Shahibaug, from Jaipur's old city to Delhi's Connaught Place — Jain communities have gathered for eight days of intense spiritual practice, prayer, fasting, and introspection.

The word Paryushan derives from Sanskrit, meaning "the soul dwelling in itself" — a time when Jain monks and nuns halt their wandering and settle in one place for the monsoon months, a tradition dating to the time of Lord Mahavira himself.

Key Events This Year

This year's Paryushan features several notable highlights. In Mumbai, leading Muni Shri Ratnasagar Maharaj is guiding a major spiritual program at the Walkeshwar Jain Derasar, attracting thousands daily. In Ahmedabad, the Shri Anandji Kalyanji Pedhi has organized a special Kalyanaka celebration on the fifth day. In Jaipur, over 200 Jains have undertaken the Attham (three-day fast with no food or water), and this number is reported to be the highest in the city's recent history.

Shvetambara and Digambara Observances

Shvetambara Jains observe Paryushan for eight days, while Digambara Jains observe it as the ten-day Das Lakshana Parva. The eight days correspond to the eight nights of the Kalpa Sutra recitation, culminating in Samvatsari — the day of universal forgiveness. The ten days of Das Lakshana each focus on one of the ten qualities of the soul: forbearance, humility, simplicity, contentlessness, truth, self-restraint, austerity, renunciation, non-possessiveness, and celibacy.

How to Observe Paryushan

  • Visit your local Jain temple daily and attend evening discourses (Pravachan)
  • Observe fasting — even partial fasting (Ekasana or Biyasana) is meritorious
  • Listen to or read the Kalpa Sutra, the biography of Mahavira
  • Practice Samayik (equanimity meditation) for 48 minutes daily
  • On Samvatsari, recite "Michhami Dukkadam" to all and seek forgiveness
  • Perform Pratikraman (confession and repentance prayer)

The Jain Diet: Why Veganism is Not Enough for a True Jain

📅 April 16, 2025 ⏱️ 8 min read By Priya Mehta, Nutritionist & Jain Scholar

When people first hear about the Jain diet, they typically assume it is simply another form of veganism — no animal products, no dairy. While this is partially correct, the Jain dietary system is a sophisticated, philosophically grounded framework that is far more nuanced than any modern diet trend. It is rooted not in health optimization, environmental concerns, or personal preference, but in one foundational principle: Ahimsa — the minimization of harm to all living beings.

Understanding the Jain diet requires first understanding the Jain classification of living beings.

The Sense-Based Classification of Life

Jain philosophy classifies all living beings (Jivas) by the number of senses they possess, from the simplest one-sensed organisms to the fully conscious five-sensed beings with a mind (like humans and animals). The more senses a being possesses, the greater its capacity for suffering, and therefore the more harmful it is to kill it.

  • One-sensed (Ekendriya) — Earth bodies, water bodies, fire bodies, air bodies, plants, and vegetation. These have only the sense of touch.
  • Two-sensed (Dvindriya) — Worms, leeches, shells. Touch and taste.
  • Three-sensed (Trindriya) — Lice, ants, moths. Touch, taste, smell.
  • Four-sensed (Chaturindriya) — Flies, mosquitoes, bees. Touch, taste, smell, sight.
  • Five-sensed without mind (Panchendriya) — Many insects, fish.
  • Five-sensed with mind (Sanjni Panchendriya) — Mammals, birds, humans.

Why Veganism Falls Short

A vegan avoids all animal products but may freely consume root vegetables. To a Jain, this misses a crucial point: root vegetables like potatoes, onions, and garlic contain anant-kayas — they host infinite numbers of one-sensed lives within a single body. Pulling a potato from the ground doesn't just kill one being; it destroys the habitat for millions of microscopic lives.

What Jains Avoid and Why

  • All meat, fish, poultry — Five-sensed beings with minds, capable of intense suffering
  • Eggs (fertilized) — Contain a living being
  • Root vegetables (potatoes, onions, garlic, carrots, beets, turnips) — Anant-kayas, infinite lives per root
  • Brinjal (eggplant) — Multi-seeded; many lives
  • Honey — Harvesting involves killing bees and larvae
  • Alcohol — Produced through fermentation involving massive microbial harm
  • Eating after sunset — Insects are attracted to light; cooking and eating at night inadvertently kills more organisms

Practical Guide for Modern Jains

In today's urban life, strict adherence to the Jain diet requires planning. Here are practical tips:

  • Replace potatoes with sweet potatoes (though some Jains avoid these too), raw banana, or yams
  • Use asafoetida (heeng) as a substitute for the flavor of onion and garlic
  • Eat your largest meal at lunchtime, before sunset
  • During Paryushan, many Jains go further and eat only boiled food, avoiding fermented or raw foods
  • Choose whole fruits over juices (juices involve more organisms in fermentation)

The Jain diet is not a restriction — it is an expression of the profound respect that Jain philosophy holds for all forms of life. As environmental consciousness rises globally, the world is slowly beginning to understand what Jains have known for millennia.
